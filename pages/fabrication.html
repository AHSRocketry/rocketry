Nose Cones

The nose is the front end of the model rocket (astounding, I know). It is made of two parts:

  • Nose
  • Shoulder

The shape of the nose can vary widely, leading to different aerodynamic profiles. Some common nose shapes are in the table below. Click on them to learn more about each nose type.

Conical

Ogive

Ellipsoid

Power Series

Parabolic

Haack Series

The nose must always be free to slide forward and come off. Therefore, the back end of the nose is cut down to form an internal shoulder which will slide inside the body tube and hold the nose in place. The base diameter of the nose should match the outside diameter of the body tube, while the diameter of the shoulder should match the inner diameter of the body tube so that it will slip-fit inside the tube. It's better to have the shoulder a little loose because you can always build it up by wrapping tape around the shoulder. If it's too large, then you'll need to sand the shoulder down. A rule of thumb for testing the looseness of the shoulder is that it should pull easily from the body tube but shouldn't rattle if you shake the body tube.

The nose must be made of plastic or balsa wood, but never metal. Nor should a metal pin or nail be attached to the tip as it could turn a malfunctioning rocket into a missile or lawn dart. Besides, rounded nose tips perform better than pointed ones.

Several commercial noses are available, and it can be very simple to select a predesigned nose and move on.

Designing one's own nose cone is a lot of fun and one of the key ways to customize a rocket. It is also one of the key components of a rocket's stability and aerodynamics. It is therefore very important that fabricated model matches the computer design.

Designing a Nose in Open Rocket

Design your nose in OpenRocket by selecting the Sustainer from the Design Tree and choosing "Nose Cone" from the Add New Component Section. Then either select from a list of pre-designed noses or close it and make your own. The things you must modify/confirm are:

    General Tab

  1. Nose cone shape: choose from the six shapes above.
  2. Nose cone shape: modify the chosen shape
  3. Length: Adjust to modify your stability or make it match the nose you are planning to use
  4. Base diameter: should match body tube's outer diameter.
  5. Wall thickness: typically 1.5mm to 2 mm.
  6. Component Material: PLA or Resin

    Shoulder Tab

  1. Diameter: match the inner diameter of tube minus 0.4 mm
  2. Length: 50% to 100% of the body tube is appropriate.
  3. Thickness: match the thickness of the nose

Body Tube

Body tubes for model rockets are usually mjade from thin walled paper tubes. Because such tubes are not easy to make, most model rocketeers buy them ready-made from hobby shops or by direct mail from manufacturers

Designing in OpenRocket

\

Body tubes are best designed in OpenRocket using built-in presets, as this will capture the proper outer and inner diameters, thickness, and material automatically. All that is left to do afterward is to adjust the length of the tube to achieve the proper centers of gravity and pressure for a good stability score. It is very important that the material of the body tube reflect reality as it is likely the largest piece of the rocket, and any incorrect materials will seriously compromise stability calculations.

Measuring & Marking

To cut a tube to the required length, first measure the desired length of tube with a steel rule or with one of the yellow marking guides. The yellow guides allow you to set a marker to the desired length and insert a pencil into the hole at one end. Then to mark the cutting line around the tube, simply twist the tube in place, allowing the pencil to scribe a perfectly flat line around the tube.

It really pays to use precision tools like this rather than eyeballing a flat line, as a messy or angled cut will prevent a nose or transition from sitting straight in the tube and could seriously impose some rotational forces on the rocket in flight.

 

To cut the tube along the scribed line, you can use cutting guides for tubes of BT60 or smaller. Take the appropriate sized cutting guide, separate it into halves and slide the part with the flat edge up to the penciled line. Then slide the other half up until it tightens the cutting guide in place.

Cut the tube with an X-acto knife. Make several passes around the tube with the blade, cutting only a little on each pass. Don't try to cut all the way through the tube on the first pass or try to stab the tip of the knife through or you'll mess up the tube. With a little patience, and with some practice, you should be able to cut a body tube so that you can't tell it was cut to custom length.

 

Fin Slots

We also need to cut slots in the body tube to allow the fins to pass through the tube wall between the motor mount centering rings. Cutting these slots also ensures the fins are attached radially and at even spacing.
